The Solar System: Our Cosmic Neighborhood

The solar system consists of the Sun and all the objects that orbit around it, including planets, moons, asteroids, and comets. At the center is the Sun, a massive ball of hot plasma that provides light and heat to the entire system. The Sun contains about 99.86% of the solar system's mass.

There are eight planets in our solar system: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. The four inner planets—Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars—are rocky and terrestrial. The four outer planets—Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune—are gas giants, composed mostly of hydrogen and helium.

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known planet to harbor life. It has a protective atmosphere, liquid water on its surface, and a magnetic field that shields it from harmful solar radiation. Mars, often called the Red Planet, has the largest volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons, and evidence of past water flow.

Jupiter is the largest planet, with a diameter about 11 times that of Earth. It has at least 95 moons, including the four largest: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto. Saturn is famous for its prominent ring system, made of ice and rock particles. Uranus and Neptune are ice giants, with atmospheres containing methane that gives them their blue color.

Beyond Neptune lies the Kuiper Belt, a region of icy bodies, and the Oort Cloud, a distant shell of icy objects. The solar system formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a collapsing cloud of gas and dust.
