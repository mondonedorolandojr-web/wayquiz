Ancient Egypt: The Land of the Pharaohs

Ancient Egypt was a civilization that flourished along the Nile River in northeastern Africa for over 3,000 years, from around 3100 BCE to 30 BCE. The Nile River was crucial to Egyptian life, providing fertile soil for agriculture through annual floods and serving as a transportation route.

Egyptian society was highly stratified, with the pharaoh at the top as both a political and religious leader. Pharaohs were considered gods on Earth and were responsible for maintaining ma'at, the cosmic order. The vizier acted as the pharaoh's chief advisor and administrator.

The Egyptians built massive pyramids as tombs for their pharaohs. The Great Pyramid of Giza, built for Pharaoh Khufu around 2580 BCE, is the largest pyramid ever constructed. It originally stood 481 feet tall and was made of over 2 million limestone blocks, each weighing about 2.5 tons.

Religion played a central role in Egyptian life. They worshipped many gods and goddesses, such as Ra (the sun god), Isis (goddess of magic and wisdom), and Osiris (god of the underworld). The afterlife was very important, and Egyptians believed in preserving the body through mummification to ensure the soul's journey to the next world.

Egyptian writing, known as hieroglyphics, was invented around 3200 BCE. It used pictures and symbols to represent words and sounds. The Rosetta Stone, discovered in 1799, helped modern scholars decipher hieroglyphics because it contained the same text in three scripts: hieroglyphic, Demotic, and Greek.

Egyptian art and architecture were highly developed. They created detailed paintings, sculptures, and jewelry. Their calendar was remarkably accurate, dividing the year into 12 months of 30 days each, plus 5 extra days.
